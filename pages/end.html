I’m so sorry, reader, it appears you can’t see my story! You can’t read the final line! You will never know how Morris’ calamity will be resolved.

That must be frustrating.

But never fear, dear reader, for you have already read a significant portion of my story.

You think you know how Morris will act. You think you understand him. After all, I am dead to you after it has been written. You have the power.

So go ahead, write that final line of Morris’ story:

Finally, Morris realised ...

Oh, what literary prowess! What a marvelous finish! Well done reader. Well done.

Or should I say author?

Who are you? You think you can just waltz in here and decide what others will think about this work! Just because you make a new meaning for yourself post-Barthe, does that really mean you can influence others about what this text really is?

Readers today play with knifes. With their Web 2.0 platforms, their tweets and blog posts. They think they can say whatever they want. They think they can create meaning for others.

Reading in today’s world is a dangerous process. You skim something, and you tweet. You like something you read and you text it to a friend. You interpret a book and you edit a wiki.

And who interprets that tweet or blog or wiki or post or text?

Readers can far too easily find themselves becoming writers - if they’re not careful.

If the author is dead, then the reader is dead as soon as they speak.

But it’s too late for you, dear reader writer. You’ve gone and done it. You haven’t just interpreted this text. You’ve rewritten it. You’re an author and you’re doomed. You have shaped the story, changed the order, rewritten the style.

Sure I left the murder weapon there. But you clicked, typed, dragged and scrolled.
