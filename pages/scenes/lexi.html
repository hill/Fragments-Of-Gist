You might get the gist of what an Author is trying to convey through semantics, but will you really understand how an Author experiences that meaning? We seem to kill some kind of intimacy when stories are told only with signs and characters.

Furthermore, as my good friend Saussure has described in Cours de Linguistique Generale, the signifier, the signified and the referent can be completely disassociated from each other. This leads us to perceive something completely different from the authorial intention.

What a pickle.

What about gestures? They provide a tool for the storyteller to apply additional semantics on top of a direct, spoken, intention. Cassell and McNeill are adamant that “Gestures are partial evidence for the shape of a speaker’s underlying linguistic processes. It gives a complete picture of the narrative event”.

So let’s have a crack at this section with some different signifiers, shall we?

McNeill and Cassell believe that while gestures enhance the semiotic value of the phrase it accompanies, they only convey the meaning of the person reciting the text.

Does that mean the person reciting a text is therefore an author, creating meaning above what is already there through the medium of gesture?

If that’s true, that would mean that you become an author whenever you recite anything.
