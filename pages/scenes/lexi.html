Chapter 6

You might get the gist of what an author is trying to convey through lexical semantics, but will you really understand how an author experiences that meaning? We seem to kill some kind of intimacy when stories are told only with signs and characters.

Furthermore, as my good friend Saussure has described in Cour de Linguistique Generale, the signifier, the signified and the referent can be completely disassociated with each other, leading us to perceive something completely different from the authorial intention.

What about gestures? They provide a tool for the storyteller to apply additional semantics on top of a direct, spoken, intention.

“Gestures are partial evidence for the shape of a speaker’s underlying linguistic processes. It gives a complete picture of the narrative event.” (Cassell and McNeill)

While gestures enhance the semiotic value of the phrase it accompanies, it only conveys the meaning of the person reciting the text. Does that mean the person reciting a text is therefore an author, creating meaning above what is already there through the medium of gesture?

Do you become an author whenever you recite anything?
