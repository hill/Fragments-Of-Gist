Chapter 3

Morris knew it was Lexi. She had a thing for not following instructions.

Morris stared at Tank’s half exposed body. He’d have to clean up the mess later. There was a much more important job to deal with now.

This woman. Morris trusted her!

How had he been so shortsighted to miss this true evil that lay in his own bed?
Now was the time to express his pain.

Morris strode out of his apartment and made a beeline for his car.

Lexi would have to feel the consequences of her actions.

What fiendish ploy does Morris have in store for his unsuspecting lover?

But I worry, dear reader. I worry that the audience may not discern the nuanced intonation that has been applied to this body of text.

I have a voice! I am a person! But this faceless slab of writing is devoid of emotion and interactivity that is inherent in face-to-face storytelling.

Barthes, Levi-Strauss, Todorov and Propp are consumed with narrative that is only defined by its basic structures - plot, characterisation and representation through language - to even consider the conversational dynamics of storytelling. Levi-Strauss’ analysis of the Oedipus myth or Propp’s dissection of Russian folklore do not examine actual discourse strategies that are so effective at conveying meaning.

But my good friend David Herman celebrates “The Performative Dimension” of storytelling: intonation, pace and tone.

As you read this story, I have no control over your internal utterances!

Sure… I italicise things for emphasis but that requires a shared understanding of what vocal stress should be applied.

Just imagine how a reader could be annunciating these words in their own heads…
