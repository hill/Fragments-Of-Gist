Good Evening,

Hello there reader. What a delightful Evening it is, don't you think?

You're sitting here. Reading this. I'm sitting here. Writing this. Doing ... whatever fictional narrators do. Is there any other place you'd rather be?

I thought not.

Oh! I almost forgot to introduce myself. My name is Alfie. And I shall be your host for this Evening.

But let's cut the chit chat shall we? What are you here for? Well, today we're going to investigate a murder.

The death of a dog.

The slaughter of communication.

The dissolution of a relationship

The Murder of Meaning

More specifically, you're going to read a story about a murder. A story that I wrote. Myself. With my own bare ... figurative hands.

You might like it. You might not. But you’re going to read it anyway.

You're going to lap up those words, those sentences, those glyphs.

You’re going to sit on that chair and be influenced! Just think: My thoughts, my imagination, my speculation: a major contributing factor to your own thinking. Marvelous.

This is the story of a character named Morris. Morris' sole purpose is to entertain you. He's pretty great like that.

Morris' name was chosen for the specific reason that it is uncommon enough (only one in eighteen thousand, two hundred and fifty eight!) in the target demographic that it might elicit some kind of memorable experience for the reader.
I have chosen that name for him.

Morris will find himself in a difficult situation. He will embark on a journey of revenge and retribution. A journey of vengeance.
I put Morris in that difficult situation.

Finally, Morris will succeed. You, dear reader, will feel a sense of great euphoria after finally receiveing resolution. Morris' quest will be complete. The story will be over.
I have decided that final line.

Morris' story is already written.
I wrote it.

And a story of such grandeur deserves a fitting title. A first impression for my newfound readers. A first glimpse at meaning.

I think I shall call it:

The Morris Chronicles

Mmmmm. No. How about:

Morris: A life and times

No. Far too vauge. How about this:

The Story of Morris, Lexi, A Dog Named Tank and Their Fantastic Adventure of IKEA Furniture, Loose Screws and Dead Pets.

Nothing up to interpretation now! They'll get the gist straight away. They'll love it. But perhaps it takes up a little too much ink. Let's truncate it:

The Story of Morris.

Brilliant. It lets the reader know exactly what this is. A story. About a man named Morris. They'll get the gist. They'll get it.

But what if they don't? What if you don't?

What if you don't love it? What if you are not entertained? What if that this is all for nothing?

I would be awfully disappointed.

Then you would not understand it.

Then you wouldn't get it.

You wouldn't get:

The Meaning

I mean. I know Barthes would be wincing at all this divine imagry and allusion to "it" as a single theological meaning. The divine and sole message of the Author-God.

But just think. All that meaning! Wasted! Lost!

Murdered.

What a tradgedy.

Oh! I know! What if ...

... and I know I'm being awfully spontaneous here ...

... what if we read the story together!

You could have input!

You could have a chance at changing the narrative.

I mean. I think it will be pretty difficult to improve upon:

The Story of Morris.

... for it is flawless. But I suppose we could at least try.

For the reader's sake.

For your sake.

For the sake of meaning!

Oh yes I like this idea. I like this idea alot. Let's read this story together! Finally, author and audience united once more! It'll be like the classical times. Like the greeks. Direct narration and interjection from the narrator.

Finally you can have your say in the story, just ...

... don't let it bite
